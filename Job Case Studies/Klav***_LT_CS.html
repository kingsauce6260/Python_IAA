4. Explain the statistical assumptions that the model makes. Do you think those assumptions are valid for a model of a customer buying widgets from an ecommerce store?

MBG Assumptions¶

Assumption 1: Each individual, I, has a hidden $lambda_i$ and $p_i$ parameter.

The first part means that each individual has a hidden rate of purchase/transaction rate, lambda(.) This assumes that each individual is different thus allowing for a distribution of s instead of an unlikely constant . The second part of the assumption states that each individual has a unique dying probability of p, instead of an unlikely constant overall p.

Does this make sense for an ecommerce widget store? Yes, each customers buying pattern, , is different and each has a different probability of death, p. It makes sense to have a distribution for shoppers transaction rates, , rather than one for everyone, and it makes sense for there to be a distribution for shoppers probabilities of death, rather than having one probability for everyone.

From here estimates of distributions can start to develop, that generate the parameters, essentially viewing customers behavior.

Assumption 2: The $lambda_i$ and $p_i$ parameters come from a population wide Gamma and a Beta Distribution respectively.

This assumption states that $lambda_i$ comes from a Gamma Distribution of the individuals lambda population. In our case the Gamma Distribution, Gamma(r, ), helps predict wait time(T) for future events, utilizing the parameters: transaction rate(where r=; following a Poisson Distribution) and number of events you are waiting to occur(where k=). It is important to note that a big attraction for the Gamma and Beta Distributions are that they have the ability to create significantly differing shapes(with only two parameters) providing flexibility.

The second part of this assumption states that $p_i$ comes from a Beta distribution of probabilities of dying. The Beta Distribution is known for its versatility in shape and can be used to adjust as new information comes in. In our case, MBG, Beta(a, b) creates a distribution of probabilities of dying.

Utilizing all parameters(,r,a,b), we can start to learn and predict more about the customers. An example would be the probability of a customer being alive given their recency, frequency, and T.

Does this make sense for an ecommerce widget store? Yes, both distributions have high flexibility with minimal inputs. It also makes sense to have transaction rates and probabilities of dying be distributions instead of one overall constant number.

Assumption 3: Individuals purchases follow a Poisson process with rate $_i$*t.

This assumptions helps predict the probability of events(purchases) happening, using the data about how often an event has occured in the past. Using lambda, , we are able to get probability for a given number of purchases for an individual in a fixed interval of time.

Does this make sense for an ecommerce widget store? Yes, Poisson Distributions are commonly used to forecast purchases on days/seasons. However, there is a concern that the is constant. As I discuss later, this may not be the best assumption if there are not two full cycles of data available or if Black Friday is not modeled seperately.

Assumption 4: At the beginning of their lifetime and after each purchase, an individual has a $p_i$ probability of dying (never buying again.)

This assumption is where the MBG model differs from the BG model. Unlike BG, the MBG model allows customers to die after their first purchase, time(T)=0. In the BG model, individuals are not allowed to die until they have a repeat purchase. This is extremely important in the widget store case because of the extremely high concentration of one time purchasers, as you can see in the graph Number of Repeat Transactions Per Customer. This graph shows frequency of purchases ie number of repurchases.

After each point in time that a purchase occurs, the probability of death for the individual is reassessed. Hence, the point at which the individual dying is now distributed across transactions, using a shifted geometric distribution. The more repurchases there are the less uncertainty of the individual dying, the probability of dying decreases. This makes sense because when given a history of customer data you are going to likely base whether a customer will repurchase on their purchase history. You will generally have more confidence they will return if they continuously repurchase, hence uncertainty will go down.

Does this make sense for an ecommerce widget store? It mostly does. Yes, because maybe if the store has a ton of different widgets and the customer is loyal to the store your uncertainty of death will decrease if their business needs multiple widgets over time. It puts me more at ease that the assumption also includes that individuals can also die at the beginning of their lifetime, which aligns more with widget buyers. There is only so many widgets an individual can buy(usually.) Playing devils advocate, one could make the case, that in most cases, the more widgets an individual buys, the less likely they are to purchase again. This is because, again, how many widgets does a company/customer actually need?

Gamma-Gamma Assumptions¶

To predict the monetary value the Gamma-Gamma model is needed. The Gamma-Gamma model can predict the most likely value per transaction in the future. Combining our MBG and GG model, I am able to build a Customer Lifetime Value(CLV) for the next 12 months.

Assumption 1: The monetary value of a customers given transaction varies randomly around their average transaction value.

This makes sense that the monetary output would include some sort of randomness and not a constant.

Does this make sense for an ecommerce widget store? Yes, if customers repurchase they will most likely hover around the mean of their average transaction value. This is especially incorporated into the widget store because widget costs do not have as a high variance compared to other product categories, being that they are all the same product, even though they may perform different tasks.

Assumption 2: Average transaction values vary across customers but do not vary over time for any given individual.

This makes sense that average transaction values will not vary over time for any given individual, because given that widgets are all under the same product category, customers may stay within a particular price limit.

An example to better explain my reasoning above. Person A does not mind spending money on food and will always pay the top price. However, Person A is very frugal when it comes to widget shopping, always looking for discounts and the best bang for buck. Compared to Person B, who is very frugal when it comes to food, always trying to be concious not to over spend. However, Person B does not mind spending a lot on widgets and wants the best for his/her website. People having different buying preferences for different product categories. Affirming that people won't vary in average transaction values over time, given a specific product category.

Does this make sense for an ecommerce widget store? Yes, widgets are a certain category so I believe consumers will continue to spend their average amount over time.

Assumption 3: The distribution of average transaction values across customers is independent of the transaction process.

This assumption was checked earlier, before fitting the Gamma-Gamma model, and it was found that there was a correlation of 0.042791 between monetary and frequency values.

Assumption 4: The Gamma-Gamma model assumes only repeat purchases in the data, meaning a frequency>=1. This assumption was met when repeat purchases were filtered before fitting the Gamma-Gamma model.

Assumption 5&6: The Gamma-Gamma model assumes the average transaction value, $z_i$, is distributed as such $z_i$ gamma(p,), with E($Z_i$|p,) = = p/. While also assuming the hyperparameter , has a distribution as such gamma(q, ).

Holdout¶

Another way to analyze how well the model performs is break the data into a calibration period dataset and a holdout dataset. This allows us to test on data not yet seen. To choose a calibration period, Jean-Rene Gauthier and Ben Van Dyke have run many simulates and they say that the typically calibration dataset should be five to ten times the typical inter purchase time of the customers. The minimum should be three times the typical inter purchase time of the customers, to get a good performance on the model. The test dataset length should be at least half the size of the training period, making sure the testing period is comparable to the training period.

This means in our one year of data, after excluding the high counts at the end that are most likely Black Friday, the minimum amount of months for the test dataset should be 4, half of 8. A discussion of why to have the end date 2017-11-27 follows.

7. What are one or two ideas that could address the deficiencies of the model? (No need to implement, just describe.)

The biggest deficiencies of the model is the lack of ability to test a whole buying cycle, in this case a year, and how our model underperforms with customers who have more repurchases.A few recommendations:
1) Collecting two years of data to get 2 full cycles.
2) Treating Black Friday and other anomolies seperately, ie maybe use a simulation model technique that models the extreme events such as Black Friday. Given enough data I would use an Exceedances technique with Generalized Pareto distribution if we wanted to get specific about those outlier dates.
3) Once the model is confirmed to be good, both training and validation can be combined and forecasted out a desired period.
4) Consider modeling for different coherts, ex. highly likely to purchase in the next year, instead of exact numbers.
5) Join with a linear model with additional features or intervention variables.

After binned into highly likely to purchase in the next year, somewhat likely to purchase in the next year, unlikely to purchase in the next year, and highly unlikely to purchase in the next year, I would perform an ANOVA test to confirm that the models can be identified by their new segmentation bases, that were used during the segmentation process. This is important because I dont want to be comparing models that binned the customers in the same way, this would give us nothing to compare.

Once segmented and depending on the type of store, compare values such as average spent per buying cycle, median products bought, quantity per order, how often they purchase, etc By using ANOVA to statistically compare these models segments you are able to statistically confirm which is better at distinguishing these groups of customers.

Here is a key to follow along with the example. Highly likely to purchase in the next year(Segment 1), somewhat likely to purchase in the next year(Segment 2), unlikely to purchase in the next year(Segment 3), and highly unlikely to purchase in the next year(Segment 4.)
